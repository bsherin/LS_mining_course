Angela Johnson Apr 17, 2018 3:12pm

It was really fun to try out true/false with my students and get to see their thinking - they had so many different ideas! But it was definitely challenging to know how to respond at times and I worry that maybe I didn't involve enough of my students. I'm looking forward to continuing with tasks like these so I can hear more students' ideas and keep working on my own facilitation of their conversations.

In terms of what students noticed and how they supported their claims, I saw a few things happening. Several of the boys who responded latched on to the idea that you can add up the twos (the divisors) to get four, so the statements are equal. I expected some students might say something like that since we’ve talked about the distributive property with multiplication. One thing that surprised me a bit was when the second student who responded said that "If you switch it around..." then it would be 80/2 + 80/2 = 80/4. It seems like he is thinking the same thing as the other boys, except I now have some questions about his understanding of equality that I'd like to explore. I wonder why he thought switching the two sides of the equation was necessary to justify his thinking that the statement is true. Any thoughts on that?

V was the first to respond for the students who thought the equation was false, and her idea was that you can't change the divisor; you can only change the dividend. She also said that if you did 80/2 you already split all the Skittles, so you can't do it again. It seems like she understands that the distributive property doesn't work in this situation, and she manages to convince J, though after watching the clip again, I'm not sure whether some of the other students understood her claim. I'm also wondering whether students might actually be more confused by her comment that you can't split it again. Technically we could split it again; we would just divide the result by 2 again, rather than adding 80/2. So in other words it would be (80/2)/2. I'm thinking now that it would have been good to unpack some of her language a bit more and hear from different students to see what they thought about V's idea. 

Overall, one of my goals is to figure out how to get a better handle on what some of the quieter students in my class are thinking. When I watched the video again, I could see a number of students who seemed disengaged, and I want to bring them in a bit more. I'm thinking that maybe I could ask students more often if they have any clarifying questions they want to ask the student who shared, or I could ask more students to repeat each other's thinking. Any other ideas for how I can bring in more voices?

Looking forward to doing this again, and can't wait to see some of your videos and hear about your experiences!

Hi Angela, I really enjoyed watching your students as all students are pretty similar across grade level! Just the way a handful of students responded with reasoning and others turned around and screamed "TRUE" made me reflect on my own primary students. It is vital to teach our students exactly how to discuss. I have often used "Talk Moves" to prompt my students - What do you think? I agree... I disagree... That might help engage your quieter students. They may not know exactly how to get involved in the conversation. It was very interesting to follow your students line of thinking - V clearly has a strong grasp on division in relation to multiplication. I think that having students discuss their understanding helps us as teachers see into their brain and grasp their conceptual understanding. Thanks for sharing!
